The Crypto Quadrilemma: Why Sustainability Matters

Decentralization, scalability, and security have long been priorities - but sustainability is often overlooked. Explore the full picture behind the cryptocurrency quadrilemma.

By ꓘlassic · Published on Jul 25, 2025

The blockchain quadrilemma refers to the profound challenge of achieving four critical aspects of blockchain technology simultaneously: security, scalability, decentralization, and sustainability. This concept suggests that optimizing one often compromises the others, making it exceedingly hard to achieve all four without significant trade-offs.

This expanded challenge builds upon the widely known blockchain trilemma, a concept first introduced by Ethereum co-founder Vitalik Buterin. The trilemma highlights the inherent difficulty in balancing security, scalability, and decentralization. While often seen as a zero-sum tradeoff, it’s important to remember that the original trilemma argued that achieving all three elements was hard, not impossible. Now, with sustainability added as a fourth, equally critical dimension, the complexity deepens. Sustainability has long been overlooked in discussions about blockchain design - frequently underprepared for and inadequately addressed. Yet as the ecosystem matures, sustainability can no longer remain an afterthought. It demands thoughtful integration alongside the original pillars, pushing the boundaries of innovation and responsible development.

Understanding the Blockchain's Essential Pillars

Let's break down what each of these four pillars means and why their harmonious coexistence is crucial for a truly robust and future-proof blockchain network.

  • Security: This refers to the robust defenses that blockchain networks must have in place to prevent malicious entities from taking over. A secure blockchain ensures the integrity and safety of its data, making it exceedingly difficult for malicious entities to alter or manipulate information.
  • Scalability: This is the network's capacity to handle a growing amount of work and its potential to be enlarged to accommodate that growth. A scalable blockchain can process a high volume of transactions quickly and efficiently, without significantly increasing costs or times.
  • Decentralization: This is a key characteristic where control over the network is equally distributed among all participants, rather than being concentrated in a single central entity. Decentralization enhances the transparency and fairness of the network, as every participant has equal access to information.
  • Sustainability: This means the network can run long-term, ensuring hardware requirements for running a node don't become too high over time, and that network fees from usage should eventually sustain the network without relying on ongoing inflation or continuous new token emissions, while also avoiding wasted energy.

Addressing the Challenges: Other Approaches and Their Issues

Many projects explore various solutions to tackle the trilemma, but these often introduce new compromises, especially when considering the fourth pillar of sustainability.

Proof of Stake systems are frequently presented as a solution for scalability, but it actually doesn’t bring any scalability improvements - only energy efficiency. Proof of Work is actually faster than PoS for consensus, as PoS has overhead. PoW removes processing time and the cycles that are required in order to facilitate consensus, resulting in faster transactions. As validators require a large amount of coins to participate - on Ethereum, this currently exceeds $100,000 - while in comparison, a PoW network like Kaspa can be mined with hardware costing under $200. This massive entry barrier gives disproportionate influence to large entities like exchanges and ETF providers, increasing the risk of collusion or coordinated control over the network. The 2020 Steemit takeover, where major exchanges used customer funds to influence network governance, vividly demonstrated how PoS centralization can play out in practice. PoS sacrifices true decentralization for energy efficiency.

Sharding is another common approach aimed at improving scalability. This process breaks down the blockchain into smaller, more manageable pieces, or "shards," with each shard capable of processing its own transactions. While this can boost throughput, it also introduces a larger number of moving parts and coordination overhead. Transactions that span multiple shards often require complex cross-shard communication protocols, which can slow down processing times and increase latency. Furthermore, with fewer nodes validating transactions within each shard, the security of individual shards may be reduced, making them more vulnerable to attack. This fragmentation has led to real-world issues - networks attempting sharding, like ICP and TON, have suffered from outages due to these complexities and vulnerabilities.

Layer 2 solutions, such as side-chains and state channels, are built on top of a main blockchain to offload transaction processing, thereby increasing scalability. While they can boost throughput, L2 solutions often introduce a degree of centralization. They frequently rely on specific operators or governance structures, creating new points of failure in the system. Moreover, L2s do not contribute to the security, decentralization, or state integrity of the base layer - they operate largely in isolation and merely settle results back to the main chain. Additionally, L2s frequently suffer from poor user experience and onboarding complexity, adding friction to adoption. A prime example is the Lightning Network, a Layer 2 solution for Bitcoin touted for enabling fast, low-fee transactions. Despite this promise, it has proven to be fundamentally insecure, unreliable, and custodial in practice - undermining key principles of trustless finance. With persistent issues like routing failures, liquidity constraints, and poor usability, the Lightning Network has now seen over two years of consistent user decline, highlighting the systemic limitations of such Layer 2 architectures.

For context, current mainstream blockchains like Bitcoin, while prioritizing security and decentralization, face significant challenges with scalability and sustainability. Their ledger size continuously increases, and they generally lack pruning mechanisms that would allow node operators to discard old, unnecessary data without compromising security. This ongoing growth can lead to increasingly high hardware requirements for running full nodes over time.

Misplaced Priorities: Beyond the Core Pillars

Some crypto projects attempt to expand the trilemma even further, incorporating ideas like privacy and interoperability as additional "lemmas." They often pursue these features without fully solving the foundational trilemma and quadrilemma challenges, which can be counterproductive. While interoperability (how different blockchains communicate) and privacy are certainly useful features, they are arguably not as fundamental to the base layer as true sustainability.

Furthermore, forcing privacy into the base layer protocol isn't always necessary or beneficial. The real power comes from giving developers the freedom to build privacy tools and applications on top of a neutral, robust base layer. Architecting privacy directly into the base layer can bring risks like regulatory delisting or suppression, and it can reduce overall trust in the project itself. A strong, neutral engine should allow for a wide range of innovations built by its community, without dictating all functionalities.

Solving the blockchain quadrilemma requires a fresh approach that fully integrates all four pillars: scalability, security, decentralization, and usability, without relying on traditional compromises. While the search for a perfect solution continues, projects are exploring innovative architectures to achieve this complex balance. One such project is Kaspa, which uses a blockDAG structure instead of a conventional blockchain to support high throughput and fast confirmation times while preserving decentralization and strong security. Its novel design offers a promising path toward resolving the fundamental tradeoffs that have long constrained blockchain systems.
